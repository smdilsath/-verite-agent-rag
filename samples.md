2.How has the reduction in cigarette tax rates affected government revenue in Sri Lanka?

Answer
Impact of the cigarette‑tax reduction on Sri Lanka’s government revenue

Aspect	What the Verité analysis found
Overall tax‑in‑price trend	Since 2020 the share of tax in the retail price of cigarettes fell sharply, dropping to 68.8 % for the most‑sold brand (MSB) and 69.9 % for the lowest‑priced brand (LPB). Both figures are below the international benchmark of 75 % and below historic Sri Lankan norms【27012025_Stealth_Reduction_Of_Cigarette_Tax_Rates.pdf, p. 2】.
Revenue loss in 2024	The decline in the tax‑in‑price translated into a loss of LKR 9.4 billion in excise‑tax revenue from the MSB alone in 2024. That amount was effectively transferred to the producer as additional profit【27012025_Stealth_Reduction_Of_Cigarette_Tax_Rates.pdf, p. 2】.
Unique decline among product categories	Cigarettes were identified as the only product in the market where tax revenue fell in 2024, while the supplier’s revenues and profits rose【27012025_Stealth_Reduction_Of_Cigarette_Tax_Rates.pdf, p. 2】.
Policy‑goal failure	The reduction in tax rates meant Sri Lanka failed to meet the two core objectives of cigarette taxation: (1) reducing consumption by keeping cigarettes less affordable, and (2) achieving the benchmark tax‑in‑price target. The revenue shortfall is a direct manifestation of that failure【27012025_Stealth_Reduction_Of_Cigarette_Tax_Rates.pdf, p. 1】.
Summary
The Verité Research “Stealth Reduction of Cigarette Tax Rates” analysis shows that the deliberate lowering of cigarette excise taxes has cost the government billions of rupees—specifically LKR 9.4 billion lost in 2024 from the most‑sold brand alone—and has made cigarettes the sole product category where tax collections declined while producer profits rose. This revenue erosion reflects a broader policy shortfall in meeting both health‑related and fiscal targets for tobacco taxation.

3.Why does the proposed 1.3% primary expenditure rule depart from economic theory and international practice?

Answer
Why the 1.3 % primary‑expenditure rule departs from economic theory and international practice

Dimension	What economic theory says	What the 1.3 % rule does	Why this is a departure
Link between spending and revenue	 Micro‑ and macro‑economic theory argue that government‑expenditure limits should be tied to revenue (or the fiscal balance). When revenue falls, spending must be adjusted to keep the budget sustainable; when revenue rises, spending can expand 【ExpenditureLimit_F.pdf, p. 1】.	 The rule caps primary expenditure at 1.3 % of GDP, independent of how much revenue the state actually collects.	 By decoupling spending from revenue, the rule ignores the fiscal‑space constraint that theory treats as essential for debt‑sustainability.
Fiscal‑rule design	 Fiscal rules are usually classified as (i) primary‑balance rules, (ii) debt‑rules, or (iii) revenue‑rules. Only a small minority of countries adopt a primary‑expenditure rule that is independent of revenue, and none set a limit as low as 1.3 % 【ExpenditureLimit_F.pdf, p. 1】.	 Sri Lanka’s proposal is a primary‑expenditure rule independent of revenue, with an exceptionally low ceiling (1.3 % of GDP).	 International practice shows that such rules are rare (10 out of 106 countries) and, when they exist, the limits are far higher. The Sri Lanka rule therefore lies outside the norm.
Policy‑goal alignment	 Fiscal theory stresses that expenditure limits should support macro‑economic stability, growth, and the provision of public goods, which requires enough fiscal space to respond to shocks and to fund productive investments 【ExpenditureLimit_F.pdf, p. 1‑2】.	 A 1.3 % cap severely restricts the government’s ability to fund health, education, infrastructure, or counter‑cyclical measures, even when revenue is sufficient.	 The rule contradicts the theoretical rationale that spending should be calibrated to revenue and economic conditions, not a fixed share of GDP.
Empirical evidence	 Successful economies (e.g., France, Japan, the UK, China) have primary‑expenditure shares well above 1 % of GDP (ranging from ~13 % to >50 % of GDP) while maintaining fiscal sustainability 【ExpenditureLimit_F.pdf, p. 1】.	 Sri Lanka would be an outlier, with a primary‑expenditure ceiling far below the lowest‑observed benchmark (e.g., Bhutan at 33.2 % of GDP).	 The rule therefore departs from the empirical norm of how primary expenditure is set in practice.
Summary
The proposed 1.3 % of GDP primary‑expenditure rule departs from both economic theory and international practice because:

It severs the link between spending and revenue, violating the core fiscal‑rule principle that expenditure must be sustainable relative to the government’s fiscal capacity.
It belongs to a very small group of primary‑expenditure rules that are independent of revenue, and its ceiling is far lower than any existing international example.
It restricts fiscal space needed for macro‑economic stability and public‑goods provision, contrary to the micro‑ and macro‑economic rationale for flexible, revenue‑linked spending limits.
These points are drawn directly from Verité Research’s “Expenditure Limit – Fiscal Rules” analysis (ExpenditureLimit_F.pdf, pages 1‑2).

4.What does the 2024 RTI proactive disclosure ranking reveal about public authorities in Sri Lanka?
Answer
What the 2024 RTI Proactive‑Disclosure ranking tells us about Sri Lanka’s public authorities

Finding	Evidence from the Verité report
A combined ranking is produced – The 2024 ranking merges the results of Section 8 (proactive‑disclosure of information) and Section 9 (content‑disclosure of RTI requests) into a single “combined ranking” that shows how each authority performs across both dimensions【data\21082025_ProactiveDisclosure.pdf, p. 22】.	
Composite scores quantify overall performance – Each authority receives a composite score (out of 100) that aggregates its Section 8 and Section 9 results. The scores make it easy to compare the overall level of transparency among agencies【data\21082025_ProactiveDisclosure.pdf, p. 70】.	
Wide variation across agencies – The table of composite scores shows a large spread: a handful of authorities score in the high‑80s/90s, indicating strong proactive‑disclosure practices, while several others fall below 50, signalling significant gaps. This variation highlights that transparency is not uniform across the public sector.	
Section 9 (content) is a key driver of the ranking – The report breaks out the Section 9 content‑disclosure results (Exhibit 6) and shows which authorities are most effective at providing the substantive information requested under the RTI Act【data\21082025_ProactiveDisclosure.pdf, p. 19】. Those with higher Section 9 scores tend to occupy the top positions in the combined ranking.	
Specific content‑disclosure patterns are identified – Exhibit 65 (page 73) details the types of content that authorities most frequently disclose (e.g., policy documents, financial statements, service‑delivery data). The analysis shows that while many agencies are good at releasing “routine” documents, they are less consistent in providing more sensitive or detailed information.	
Overall picture – The 2024 proactive‑disclosure ranking demonstrates that Sri Lanka’s public authorities are at different stages of RTI implementation. A minority of agencies have built robust systems that meet both the proactive‑disclosure (Section 8) and content‑disclosure (Section 9) requirements, whereas a larger group still needs to improve its processes, especially in the area of responding to specific RTI requests.	
Summary
The 2024 RTI proactive‑disclosure ranking, as compiled by Verité Research, provides a single, composite view of how well each Sri Lankan public authority complies with the Right‑to‑Information Act. By combining Section 8 and Section 9 results, the ranking highlights significant disparities: some agencies achieve high transparency scores, while many lag behind, particularly in delivering the detailed content requested under Section 9. This evidence points to the need for targeted reforms to raise the overall level of proactive disclosure across the public sector.

5.What were the findings of the forensic audit regarding losses to the Employees’ Provident Fund (EPF)?
Answer
Findings of the forensic audit on losses to the Employees’ Provident Fund (EPF)

Finding	Details (as reported by Verité Research)	Source
Partial enumeration of EPF losses – The audit quantifies a set of losses that can be traced directly from corrupt or irregular transactions, but it acknowledges that many “bad‑faith” or corrupt dealings are not captured by a standard audit and therefore the listed losses are only a partial picture of the total damage to the EPF.	“The losses listed in the forensic audits … should be read as a partial, and not full, enumeration of losses caused to the EPF …”	Forensic Audit of Central Bank (2019): Assessment of Losses to the EPF, p. 17
Losses from unlisted equity investments – Between 1998 and 2017 the EPF invested LKR 11,042 million in 17 unlisted companies. 97.5 % of these investments were made after 2008. While pre‑2007 investments (LKR 278 million) generated positive dividends and capital gains, post‑2007 investments largely produced losses and, in many cases, no returns at all.	“The practice accelerated from 2008‑2017 … In contrast, investments made from 2007 onwards largely failed to produce returns and often resulted in losses.”	Forensic Audit of Central Bank (2019): Assessment of Losses to the EPF, p. 14
Specific loss figures for listed companies – The audit identified loss‑making positions in several listed firms (e.g., Weligama Hotel Properties Ltd. and Jetwing Symphony Ltd.) that together contributed LKR 389 million in capital‑gain losses.	“Name of the company … 16 Weligama Hotel Properties Limited … 17 Jetwing Symphony Limited … Total (389)”	Forensic Audit of Central Bank (2019): Assessment of Losses to the EPF, p. 21
Limitations of the forensic audit – The audit covered only a limited time span and used a methodology that could not capture all losses, especially those arising from weak governance or corrupt management that are not evident through standard audit trails. Consequently, the reported losses under‑state the true financial impact on the EPF.	“The losses listed … should be read as a partial, and not full, enumeration … The audit … has limitations of the duration covered and the methodology followed.”	Forensic Audit of Central Bank (2019): Assessment of Losses to the EPF, p. 2 & p. 17
Summary
Verité’s forensic audit of the Central Bank (2019) finds that the EPF suffered significant, but incompletely quantified, losses:

Direct losses from identified corrupt transactions (partial enumeration).
Large losses from post‑2007 unlisted equity investments (LKR ≈ 11 billion invested, most of which under‑performed).
Additional capital‑gain losses of LKR 389 million from specific listed companies.
The report stresses that because of methodological and temporal constraints, the figures presented represent only a portion of the total loss incurred by the EPF.
